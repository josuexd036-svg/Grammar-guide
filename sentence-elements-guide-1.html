Chapter 1

Sentence Elements

Every sentence is built from specific functional parts. Identifying these elements allows you to analyse any sentence — from the simplest to the most complex — with precision.

Complete Subject
CS
The entire subject of the sentence — the main noun plus all its modifiers.
The dense rainforests of Costa Rica attract millions of visitors.
Simple Subject
SS
The key noun or pronoun in the complete subject — stripped of all modifiers.
The dense rainforests of Costa Rica attract millions of visitors.
SS → rainforests
Compound Subject
CpS
Two or more subjects sharing the same verb, joined by a conjunction.
Belize and Guatemala both offer exceptional wildlife tours.
Complete Predicate
CP
Everything in the sentence that says something about the subject — verb plus all complements and modifiers.
The guide led the tourists through the Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve.
Simple Predicate
SP
The main verb (or verb phrase) of the sentence only.
The guide led the tourists through the forest.
SP → led
Compound Predicate
CpP
Two or more verbs sharing the same subject, joined by a conjunction.
Visitors hike the trails and photograph the wildlife every morning.
Direct Object
DO
The noun that directly receives the action of a transitive verb. Answers whom? or what?
The park ranger showed the monkeys to the visitors.
Indirect Object
IO
The noun that tells to whom / for whom the action is done. Appears between verb and direct object.
The agency gave the tourists a detailed map of the reserve.
Object Complement
OC
A noun or adjective that follows the direct object and renames or describes it.
Locals consider the quetzal the symbol of freedom.
Subject Complement / Predicate Nominative
SC/PN
A noun that follows a linking verb and renames the subject.
Costa Rica is a leader in sustainable tourism.
Subject Complement / Predicate Adjective
SC/PA
An adjective that follows a linking verb and describes the subject.
The cloud forest is breathtaking at sunrise.

Chapter 2

Types of Verbs

Verbs are the engine of every sentence. The most important distinction for sentence analysis is whether a verb is transitive or intransitive.

Transitive Verb

Requires a direct object to complete its meaning. The action passes from the subject to something or someone.

Formula: Subject + Transitive Verb + Direct Object

  • Tourists photograph the spider monkeys. ✔
  • Belize protects its barrier reef. ✔
  • The guide showed the group the nesting turtles. ✔
  • Panama attracts birdwatchers from around the world. ✔

Intransitive Verb

Does not take a direct object. The action is complete within the subject — or may be followed only by adverbs/prepositional phrases.

Formula: Subject + Intransitive Verb (+ modifiers)

  • The quetzal disappeared into the canopy. ✔
  • Tourists arrived at the Osa Peninsula. ✔
  • The waterfall flows year-round. ✔
  • Many species thrive in protected reserves. ✔

Linking Verbs (Special Category)

Linking verbs connect the subject to a subject complement — either a predicate nominative (noun) or predicate adjective. Common linking verbs: be, seem, appear, become, feel, look, remain, smell, sound, taste.

The Monteverde ReserveSubject   remainsLinking Verb   one of the most biodiverse on EarthSC/PN

Same Verb — Two Roles

Many verbs can be either transitive or intransitive depending on context:

Transitive:
She runs an ecotourism programme in Nicaragua.
(runs → what? → programme ✔)
Intransitive:
She runs through the rainforest each morning.
(runs → no object ✔)

Answer Key

Complete Answers

📋 Exercise A — Sentence Elements

1
SP (Simple Predicate) — "has established" is the main verb phrase. Costa Rica = SS / CS. "more than 30 national parks and protected areas" = DO.
2
CpS (Compound Subject) — "Belize and Honduras" are two subjects sharing the verb "attract." "coral reefs" = DO.
3
IO (Indirect Object) — "local communities" answers to whom the government gave the incentives. DO = "economic incentives."
4
SC/PN (Subject Complement / Predicate Nominative) — "a vital source of national income" follows the linking verb "is" and renames the subject.
5
SC/PA (Subject Complement / Predicate Adjective) — "fresh" follows the linking verb "smells" and describes the subject "cloud forest."
6
CpP (Compound Predicate) — "hike the trails and photograph the wildlife" are two verbs (+ objects) sharing the subject "Visitors."
7
OC (Object Complement) — "one of the most biodiverse places on Earth" follows the direct object (implied: "Osa Peninsula") and renames it after the verb "considered."
8
CS (Complete Subject) — "The experienced wildlife photographer" is the full subject. SS = "photographer."

📋 Exercise B — Transitive / Intransitive

1
I (Intransitive) — "nest" has no direct object; turtles simply perform the action. "on the Caribbean coast" is a prepositional phrase, not a DO.
2
T (Transitive) — DO = "nesting sea turtles." The park protects what? → turtles.
3
I (Intransitive) — "fly" has no direct object. "over the Carara National Park" is a prepositional phrase of place.
4
T (Transitive) — DO = "pristine lakes, volcanoes, and cloud forests." Nicaragua offers what? → those things. (Note: "travellers" = IO.)
5
I (Intransitive) — "migrate" has no direct object. "through the waters…" is a prepositional phrase of path.
6
T (Transitive) — DO = "the Darién Gap." Panama declared what? → the Darién Gap. "a UNESCO World Heritage Site" = OC (object complement).

📋 True or False — Answers

1
TRUE — In the sentence "Costa Rica protects its forests," "protects" is a transitive verb with DO "forests."
2
FALSE — A compound subject uses two or more subjects, not two verbs. Two verbs = compound predicate.
3
TRUE — "is" is a linking verb; "a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve" follows it and renames the subject, making it a SC/PN.
4
FALSE — "Arrived" is intransitive. You cannot arrive something; the verb needs no direct object.
5
TRUE — "tourists" answers to whom the guide sent the information → it is the indirect object.
6
FALSE — The simple subject is only the key noun: quetzal. The complete subject is "The resplendent quetzal."
7
TRUE — "disappeared" is intransitive: the quetzal disappeared into the canopy (prepositional phrase, not DO).
8
FALSE — Predicate adjectives describe the subject (e.g., "The reef looks fragile"). Predicate nominatives rename it (e.g., "The reef is a treasure").
9
TRUE — "named" is transitive (DO = "the reserve") and "symbol of biodiversity" completes the meaning of the DO → OC.
10
TRUE — Panama and Belize are two separate subjects joined by "and," making them a compound subject.
